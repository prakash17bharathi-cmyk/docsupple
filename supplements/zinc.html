Zinc

← Back to Supplements

What is Zinc?

Zinc is an essential trace mineral that plays vital roles in immune function, protein synthesis, wound healing, cell growth, and DNA synthesis. It is critical for proper functioning of immune cells and is required for numerous enzymatic processes. Unlike some minerals, your body cannot store zinc, so consistent dietary intake is essential.

Common Uses

  • Supporting immune system function
  • Supporting wound healing and tissue repair
  • Supporting healthy skin
  • Supporting eye health
  • Supporting hair and nail health
  • Promoting protein synthesis
  • Supporting taste and smell perception

Who May Benefit?

Zinc supplementation may be beneficial for:

  • People with low dietary zinc intake
  • Vegetarians and vegans (plant-based zinc is less bioavailable)
  • Older adults
  • People with certain digestive conditions
  • Those with frequent immune challenges
  • Individuals with slow wound healing
  • Those with skin health concerns

Possible Side Effects

  • Nausea (especially on empty stomach)
  • Copper deficiency with excessive long-term supplementation
  • Metallic taste
  • Headache
  • Interactions with certain antibiotics and medications
  • Excessive doses may impair immune function (paradoxical effect)

Forms & Dosage

Zinc is available in various forms:

  • Zinc gluconate (good absorption)
  • Zinc citrate (good absorption)
  • Zinc picolinate (well-absorbed)
  • Zinc acetate
  • Zinc oxide (less bioavailable)

Recommended daily intake: 8-11 mg for most adults. Supplemental doses typically range from 15-30 mg daily.

Dietary Sources

Zinc is found in:

  • Oysters and shellfish (highest sources)
  • Beef and pork
  • Chickpeas and legumes
  • Cashews and nuts
  • Seeds (pumpkin, sunflower)
  • Whole grains
  • Dairy products

Important Notes

Excess zinc can interfere with copper absorption and impair immune function. Zinc lozenges should not be used long-term. High doses may inhibit the absorption of other minerals like iron and calcium.
