Omega-3 Fatty Acids

← Back to Supplements

What are Omega-3 Fatty Acids?

Omega-3 fatty acids are polyunsaturated fats essential for numerous bodily functions. The three main types are ALA (alpha-linolenic acid), EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid), and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid). These are considered "essential" because your body cannot produce them, so they must be obtained through diet or supplements.

Common Uses

  • Supporting cardiovascular and heart health
  • Supporting brain and cognitive function
  • Promoting healthy inflammatory response
  • Supporting eye health
  • Supporting joint health
  • Supporting mental health and mood

Who May Benefit?

Omega-3 supplementation may be beneficial for:

  • People with low fish consumption
  • Individuals with cardiovascular concerns
  • People looking to support cognitive function
  • Those with joint or inflammatory concerns
  • Vegans and vegetarians (for EPA/DHA specifically)
  • Individuals with certain health conditions

Possible Side Effects

  • Fishy aftertaste or burps
  • Mild digestive upset (nausea, diarrhea)
  • Blood thinning effects at high doses
  • Potential interactions with blood-thinning medications
  • Possible increased bleeding risk if combined with anticoagulants

Forms & Dosage

Omega-3 supplements are available as:

  • Fish oil (EPA and DHA from fish)
  • Algae oil (vegetarian source of EPA and DHA)
  • Flaxseed (ALA from plant sources)
  • Krill oil (EPA and DHA from krill)

Typical doses range from 1,000-3,000 mg of combined EPA and DHA daily. Consult a healthcare provider for personalized recommendations.

Dietary Sources

Omega-3s are naturally found in:

  • Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines, trout)
  • Fish oils
  • Flaxseeds and flaxseed oil
  • Chia seeds
  • Walnuts
  • Seaweed and algae
