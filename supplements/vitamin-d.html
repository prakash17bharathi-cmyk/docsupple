Vitamin D

← Back to Supplements

What is Vitamin D?

Vitamin D is a fat-soluble vitamin that acts as both a nutrient and a hormone in the body. It's unique because your body can produce it when sunlight exposure triggers synthesis in the skin. Vitamin D plays a critical role in calcium absorption, immune system function, and cell growth regulation.

Common Uses

  • Supporting bone and dental health
  • Enhancing calcium absorption
  • Supporting immune system function
  • Promoting muscle function
  • Supporting mood and mental health
  • Maintaining cardiovascular health

Who May Benefit?

Vitamin D supplementation may be beneficial for:

  • People living in northern climates with limited sun exposure
  • Individuals who spend most time indoors
  • People with darker skin tones (higher melanin reduces vitamin D synthesis)
  • Older adults with reduced sun exposure
  • Those with certain digestive conditions affecting fat absorption
  • People with strict sunscreen use

Possible Side Effects

  • Generally safe at recommended doses
  • Excessive supplementation may cause hypercalcemia (elevated calcium)
  • Symptoms of overdose: nausea, vomiting, weakness, bone pain
  • Potential interactions with certain medications

Forms & Dosage

Vitamin D is available as:

  • Vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol, from plant sources)
  • Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol, from animal sources or synthesis)

Recommended daily intake: 600-800 IU for most adults. Some individuals may benefit from higher doses; consult a healthcare provider for personalized recommendations.

Dietary Sources

Vitamin D is found in:

  • Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
  • Fish liver oils
  • Egg yolks
  • Fortified milk and milk alternatives
  • Fortified cereals
  • Mushrooms exposed to sunlight

Sun Exposure

10-30 minutes of midday sun exposure several times per week can help maintain adequate vitamin D levels for most people.
